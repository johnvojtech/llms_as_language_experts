Guidelines for Unified Morph Segmentation in Multiple Languages
1 Intro

The aim of the project is to investigate the internal structure of words in individual languages and to
compare this structure across languages. 
Within the project, we adopt the traditional distinction between morpheme and morph. A morpheme is understood as the smallest linguistic unit that links form and meaning. A morpheme is instantiated in actual words by a morph. Some morphs may occur independently as mono-morphemic words, while others appear only in combination with other morphs and thus constitute parts of multi-morphemic words. If a single morpheme has multiple formally distinct realizations, this is referred to as allomorphy. In the project, we proceed bottom-up, that is, by identifying morphs in the attested linguistic material.
The assignment of morphs to morphemes as more abstract units will be addressed in the next phase of the project; essentially, it will involve a process analogous to lemmatization, whereby an inflected word form is linked to its dictionary form.

2 Basic principles of morph segmentation

We will work with authentic linguistic material. The task will be to segment word forms extracted from text corpora into morphs.
The basic principles for the planned morph segmentation are as follows:

• Maximum segmentation:
The goal is to find all possible morphs in a segmented word form, i.e., to completely decompose
the word into morphs ((1), (2)). Unlike other projects, such as UniMorph, the task is not to identify
only the inflectional formant (divide the inflected form into the base and inflectional ending as in
teacher + s and opravář + i), or to find only the last added derivational affix (as in teach + er and
oprav + ář).
(1) ENG teach + er + s
(2) CES o + prav + ář + i

• Delimitation of morphs in corpus-attested forms:
Morphs will be identified in word forms as they appear in text corpora. The project does not aim
at modeling word-formation processes, where a motivating word is sought for a more complex
word.

• Morph as a re-occurring unit:
Morphs are expected to re-occur in multiple different word forms. Comparing forms in these
sets—ideally consisting of at least three members—is the basic principle for identifying morphs.
For example, by comparing the words teacher, teaching, and teachable, the morph teach is de-
fined; by comparing teacher, writer, and officer defines the morpheme er. Based on the compari-
son of the English words reduction, deduction, induction, the root duc is delimited along with the
other segments shown in (3); cf. (4) for the segmentation of the Czech counterpart words.
(3) ENG re + duc + tion, de + duc + tion in + duc + tion
(4) CES re + duk + c + e, de + duk + c + e, in + duk + c + e

• Cranberry morphs vs. no segmentation
If, based on analogy, it is possible to distinguish only some morphs in a word, the remaining
substring can also be considered a morph (cranberry morph); cf. cran and huckle along with other
words containing berry in (5)). Although the words cran and huckle do not occur in other words
today, they are considered to be diachronically existent and synchronically contrasting with other
components that combine with berry.
(5) ENG cran + berry, huckle + berry, straw + berry, black + berry
On the contrary, the noun woman is not broken down into morphemes (is mono-morphemic),
because the substring wo, which would remains after identifying man, is synchronically unrecog-
nizable.

• Formal and semantic criteria:
Although a morph is considered a formal unit, we also use semantic criteria to delimit it. It
should be a string that can be associated with some (lexical, derivational, inflectional) meaning.
The formal similarity of the substring room in mushroom and the substring in bedroom, etc. is
coincidental, and mushroom should not be segmented.
Similarly, even if the nouns sister, brother, mother, father do all share the final substring er, these
words are not split in morphs, because this substring is different from er in agent nouns, and
neither it nor the remaining substrings (sist-, etc.) are available to today’s speakers.
In Czech, words such as medvˇed or manˇzel will also remain unsegmented.

• Free and bound morphs:
The separated substrings do not have to exist as separate words. The verbs in (6) are segmented
even if mit does not exist as a word.
(6) ENG sub + mit, ad + mit, o + mit

4 Simple examples
While in English we encounter mono-morphemic words (simplexes) across all parts of speech, in Ger-
man or Czech most verb forms, including infinitives, are multi-morphemic (complex; (7) and (8)).
(7) ENG run
(8) GER lauf + en
In this segmentation project, we do not work with the concept of zero morphs, i.e., morphs with
a graphically unrealized form. some morphological approaches posit zero morphs in positions where
related forms show an overt exponent. For example, a zero inflectional morph could be assumed for
the nominative singular of the Czech noun pán, since other case forms have overt endings (cf. genitive
singular pán + a or dative plural pán + ˚um); similarly, by analogy with English agent nouns formed with
the suffix er, a zero derivational morph could be posited in the noun cook. In our project, however, we
treat both pán and cook as mono-morphemic words.
Proper names may also appear in the data.1 In the project, we want to treat them similarly to common
names: their internal structure is analyzed if it is synchronously apparent.

5 More challenging examples

5.1 Allomorphy
(42) ENG put, putt + ing
(43) ENG lady, ladie + s
In (44), the simplex noun (bare root) list reappears as a root morph with a lengthened vowel in lístek
and in lísteček, where the suffix -eč is an allomorph of -ek from lístek.
(44) CES list, líst + ek, líst + eč + ek
Some letters can be considered both the final element of one morph and the initial letter of the
following morph (cf. e in writer). However, since in this step we only divide attested word forms into
substrings, the disputed letter cannot be placed in both morphs at the same time. In such cases, we assign
the disputed letter to the second morph, which may increase the allomorphy of the first morph (cf. the
root writ in writer in (45) has a different form than in the mono-morphemic infinitive write).
(45) ENG writ + er, writ + ing, write
(46) ENG happi + er, happi + ness, happy
(47) DEU Woch + en, Woch + en + ende, Woche
Contracted forms are also merely divided into segments belonging to originally separate words, but
the full form of these words is not reconstructed.
(48) ENG can + ’t, I + ’ve
(49) DEU an + s, a + m, dr + in
(50) SPA a + l, d + el
(51) CES při + běh + l + a + s
Abbreviations are also segmented if the internal structure is transparent:
(52) ENG et + c.
(53) DEU u + s + w.
(54) CES a + t + d., E + U, O + D + S

5.2 Allomorphy instead of introflection
Internal modification (vowel and consonant alternations) that might happen inside the roots are not a
reason for root segmentation, even though some concepts proceed in this way (discontinuous stems).
Here, these roots are understood as allomorphs.
(55) ENG foot, feet
(56) ENG sheep, shep + herd
(57) ENG aus + schliess + en, Aus + schluss, aus + ge + schloss + en
(58) CES vy + br + a + t, vý + běr, vý + bor

5.3 Segmentation of pronominal words
Pronouns and other pronominal expressions should be segmented only to the extent that the identified
morphs are synchronically attested across multiple forms in the language.
(59) ENG some + thing
(60) ENG no + body
(61) DEU je + mand
(62) CES ně + kdo
(63) CES ni + komu
Synchronically non-transparent pronominal (personal, possessive, demonstrative or interrogative)
substrings are not expected to be delimited at this stage of the project, even though they recur across
forms; cf.:
• ENG: th in them, their, h in he, him, his
• GER: d in das, der or in du, dich, deine

5.4 Segmentation of numeral expressions
Similarly, numerals are analyzed internally only when their structure is synchronically transparent.
(64) ENG eleven
(65) ENG two, twelve, twelf + th
(66) ENG three, thir + teen, thir + ty, thir + tie + th
5.5 Segmentation of non-inflected word classes
(67) ENG be + cause
(68) GER des + wegen
(69) CES pro + to + že
Words that function as both inflected and uninflected parts of speech (e.g., nouns and prepositions)
are segmented identically in both functions.
(70) GER mittel + s
(71) CES kol + em

5.6 Segmentation of loanwords
In the data used for segmentation, which are randomly sampled from frequency lists extracted from the
InterCorp corpora, loanwords are not distinguished from native (inherited) words. In the segmentation
process, we aim to treat loanwords as much like native words as possible (identifying inflectional end-
ings, comparing structure across sets sharing the same substring, etc.), while still respecting the structure
of the current language. We do not impose identical structural analyses of corresponding words across
different languages.
